Genetic Analysis of ErbB Signaling in C. Elegans

Institution: California Institute of Technology
Investigator(s): Nadeem  Moghal , Ph.D. -
Award Cycle: 2001 (Cycle VII) Grant #: 7FB-0103 Award: $85,633
Award Type: Postdoctoral Fellowship
Research Priorities
Pathogenesis>Too much cell growth: defective messages and internal signaling



Initial Award Abstract (2001)
In mammals, the ErbB family of genes controls the development of breast tissue. The protein product from these genes form surface membrane receptors that relay growth signals into cells. In many cases of breast cancer, mutations in either ErbB or other genes cause ErbB proteins to become too active or increase in amount. This contributes to the malignancy of the disease by stimulating uncontrolled cell growth. Drugs, such as Herceptin, inhibit ErbB2 (also called Her-2/neu) and are used to block these effects. However, blocking ErbB2 appears to be effective less than half the time. Thus, more information is needed to locate and study the functions of genes that interact with ErbB genes or produce proteins that modulate ErbB2 signaling effects. To rapidly identify and analyze potential human genes that regulate ErbB genes and their protein product, we have chosen the nematode worm, C. elegans, as a model system. C. elegans has one ErbB gene, called let-23, which controls the amount of vulval tissue in these animals. In an analogous manner to the situation in breast cancer, worms that have too much let-23 activity develop too much vulval tissue. This gives us a system and a phenotypic read-out to find other nematode genes that influence let-23.

In preliminary studies we used a chemical to randomly create mutations in C. elegans, some of which were observed to increase the amount of let-23-dependent vulval tissue. The focus of this project is to determine the identity of two such mutant genes, ear-2 and ear-1, and to characterize how their mutation increases ErbB activity. Most experiments will involve mating worms to each other to create different genetic backgrounds, and using microscopy to examine the amount of vulval tissue in these animals. Some experiments will involve the introduction of recombinant DNA into worms, the use of a laser to surgically kill specific cells, and the analysis of worm DNA for mutations. Since C. elegans has a very short life cycle, this project will allow the rapid generation and identification of mutant genes that increase sensitivity to an ErbB family member. Furthermore, different combinations of mutations can be readily created to examine how they increase or decrease ErbB activity.

We are encouraged to attempt this project, because previous work in C. elegans led to the first demonstration that the Cbl class of proteins that block ErbB activity. This relationship has now been confirmed in human cells, and importantly, the amount of Cbl protein influences the response of breast cancer cells to therapeutic agents. It might seem surprising that worms can provide a potential model for key events in breast cancer. However, it remains true that at fundamental levels of cell organization and growth control processes, worms and humans share common genes. If successful, this work will translate to a better understanding and treatment of human breast cancer.


Final Report (2003)
Introduction: For our research we use a nematode worm, called C. elegans. These animals might seem to be inappropriate model systems to study breast cancer, but surprisingly they have very similar mechanisms of cell growth control that might uncover molecular and gene interactions critical to human disease. In mammals, the ErbB family of genes regulates the development of breast tissue. In many cases of breast cancer, mutations in either ErbB family members or other genes cause ErbB (e.g., Her-2) proteins to become too active. This contributes to the malignancy of the disease. Anti-ErbB drugs, such as Herceptin, are used to block these effects, but to improve therapies, it is necessary to determine the nature of these mutations and how they increase ErbB activity. In this fellowship project, the nematode worm, C. elegans, was used as a model system to study how ErbB activity is controlled. C. elegans has one ErbB gene, let-23, which controls the amount of vulval tissue in these animals. Worms that have too much LET-23 activity develop too much vulval tissue. A chemical was used to randomly create mutations in C. elegans, some of which were observed to increase the amount of vulval tissue. Mutations disrupting the function of a gene would be analogous to mutations in human tumor suppressor genes. Mutations that increase the activity of a gene would be similar to mutations in human oncogenes, which promote tumor formation.

Topic addressed: The focus of this proposal was to determine the molecular identity of some of the chemically-induced mutations (ear-1, ear-2, and ear-3), and to study how these mutant genes, as well as other mutations, increase LET-23 activity.

Progress toward specific aims: The key aims of the original proposal were accomplished. The molecular properties of the LET-23 regulatory genes, ear-2 and ear-3, mutations were determined. The ear-2 mutations disrupt the function of a gene called dpy-22, which is most similar to TRAP230 in humans. This gene regulates the processing of information in cell nuclei. The ear-3 mutations affect the let-23 gene itself, and make the LET-23 protein abnormally active. A third gene, called egl-30, which is most similar to a protein called G?q in humans was also studied. Excessive EGL-30 activity in neurons and muscles appears to cause an increase in WNT activity in the epithelial cells that respond to LET-23. This increase in WNT activity appears to enhance the cell’s response to LET-23. WNTs are also known to promote tumor formation in humans.

Impact: This work has provided one new candidate (TRAP230) for a gene whose mutation might contribute to the development of breast cancer. It also has defined new mutations that might generally increase ErbB activity and also could contribute to breast cancer. In addition, this work has demonstrated that in a whole organism setting, abnormal gene in activity in other tissues, such as neurons and muscles can contribute to hypersensitive ErbB responses in epithelial cells. Such communication between cells may play a role in breast cancer, as well. Our research also provides new information regarding the basic mechanisms by which an organism regulates ErbB activity. Work in all of these areas has resulted in four publications this year, which are shown below and can be referenced to read about further details of these studies. In summary, our expectation that studying C. elegans would uncover potentially important clues for the regulation of the human breast cancer Her-2 gene/protein were realized.

Future direction: The CBCRP funding supported completion of my fellowship and a new faculty appointment at the Huntsman Cancer Institute, University of Utah. I plan to continue my work on ErbB proteins. Some of this work will include studying whether TRAP230 is a potential tumor suppressor gene in human cancer, and defining the biochemical effects of the let-23 mutations on ErbB activity. More work will also be done to study how neurons and muscles cooperate with WNTs to regulate epithelial responses to ErbB activity in a whole animal setting.
