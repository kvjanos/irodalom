Abstract

The Challenges for Molecular Imaging in Biomedical Research

 

The rapid advances in molecular genetics, the need to systematically examine the chemical and cellular networks in the intact living organism and the increased

emphasis to be able to observe the molecular functionalities and dynamics in living systems brought about a new surge in what is described as molecular imaging. Methods have been available for many years but the need to obtain meaningful in vivo quantitative measurements brought many new challenges.

 

The biological challenge is to select the most appropriate target for observation which varies from Genes (DNA) to message (RNA), gene product (protein) to functionality.

The Physics and Engineering challenge is to devise more and more sensitive detection methods of a variety of signals with high resolution and sufficient penetration into biological objects. The challenge to the chemists is to design specific molecules that can probe, specify and amplify the underlying biological information. Material scientists are called upon to meet the challenge of inventing new constructions of particles such as nanoparticles that can be used in a novel way as biological detectors or for delivering biomarkers to specific sites.

Patterns of Gene Expression (detected by optical means) interact with and depend on the metabolic and energetic state of the organism (observed by Magnetic Resonance Imaging and Spectroscopy). This way we can begin to describe and put together the information that is associated with a loss of control and hence results in disease. Thus the basic science discoveries can be taken to the clinic using molecular imaging techniques. We need better and more sensitive and specific measurements and one of such advances, hyperpolarised 13-C MR spectroscopy and imaging. There can be no better examples where the interaction between the physical and biomedical sciences is more appropriate than in the field of molecular bioimaging.

 

The mission of SBIC is to harness existing imaging expertise and capabilities in Singapore and develop them into a focused national platform to support the growth of multi-disciplinary research activities and speed the development and translation of biomedical research discoveries into clinical applications.

Assistant Professor, Department of Biological Sciences, National University of Singapore

Wed 30th Jan 1045-1130

 

Abstract

Precise pathways of transport maternal antibody across the epithelial barriers of suckling rat small intestine revealed with cellular electron tomography

 

Prior to development of a fully functional immune system, mammalian young depend on maternal IgG transported across epithelial barriers by the neonatal Fc receptor (FcRn). In newborn rodents, FcRn transfers IgG in milk from the apical side of intestinal epithelial cells to the basolateral surface, where it enters the blood. Here we used emerging cellular electron tomography (CET) [1] to derive a 3D map of transport vesicles in neonatal intestinal epithelial cells at 4-6nm resolution. Suckling rats were fed Fc labeled with 1.4nm Nanogold, which was enlarged in high pressure frozen/freeze substitution fixed intestinal samples to enable identification of individual FcRn ligands (Fig-1) [2-3]. Tomograms of epithelial cells revealed Fc in coated pits at the apical surface, inside tubular and irregular vesicles that formed a tangled web associated with microtubules, in multivesicular bodies (MVBs), and in clathrin-coated vesicles and coated pits near and at the basolateral surface. The transcytosis pathways inferred from these snapshots of dynamic events include unanticipated features, such as association with MVB inner vesicles and MVB tubules and transit through pleomorphic clathrin-coated tubules and vesicles.

Motomi Osato, MD, PhD

Research Assistant Professor, Institute of Molecular and Cell Biology, Oncology Research Institute, National University of Singapore

Wed 30th Jan 1400-1445

 

Abstract

Stem cell exhaustion and leukemogenesis The advantage of FACS sorting in stem cell and cancer biology

 

The RUNX1/AML1 gene encodes a transcription factor which is critical for the generation and maintenance of hematopoietic stem cells and is the most frequently targeted gene in human leukemia. Loss of function of RUNX1 predisposes cells to leukemia, and with the acquisition of cooperating genetic alterations, the cells become fully leukemogenic. Conditional deletion of Runx1 in adult mice results in an increase of hematopoietic stem/progenitor cells which serves as the target cell pool for leukemia. However, Runx1-/- mice do not develop spontaneous leukemia, probably due to the phenomenon called exhaustion. Bone marrow transplantation experiments showed that Runx1-/- stem cell maintenance was compromised, resulting in decreasing contribution of Runx1-/- stem cells to blood cell production. The development of leukemia from stem cells harboring property of exhaustion may therefore require additional genetic alterations that prevent exhaustion. We employed retroviral insertional mutagenesis (RIM) on conditional Runx1 knockout mice and have identified additional genetic alterations that cooperate with loss of function of Runx1 in leukemogenesis.

 

Biography

As a hematologist, Motomi has heavily used flow cytometry for the past 15 years.  He has experienced almost all line-up of applications and models of BD FACS machines (FACStar to FACS Aria).  Since 2002, he has provided service of FACS sorting and stem cell analyses, e.g. side population, for IMCB users using FACS Vantage. He has been studying the molecular mechanism of leukemogenesis by Runx and related genes, using cell biological approaches, in vivo mouse and zebrafish systems and analysis of human leukemia samples. His current interest is to elucidate the involvement of Runx1 and associated genes in leukemia stem cells.

Carol Tang, PhD

Principal Investigator, Neuro-Oncology Research Lab, National Neuroscience Institute

Wed 30th Jan 1445-1530

 

Abstract

Cancer Stem Cells

 

Over the years, flow cytometry has revealed a series of attractive features that bring to mind the genius of the short prose narrative Italo Calvino and his meditations on the art of writing, as lightness, quickness, exactitude, visibility and multiplicity. Flow cytometry is an exact technology because the analytical process is highly controlled and data are quantitatively expressed. It is visible, for the reason that a great part of analysis is based on pattern recognition and eyeballing of flow cytometry dot, density and contour plots to identify abnormalities. Finally, multiplicity is a progressively growing feature of flow cytometry, as polychromatic analysis has reached the level of 6 colors for routine analysis, 8 for minimal residual disease studies and up to 17 in more experimental settings. Flow cytometry is a sort of single-cell proteomics, owing to its ability to simultaneously detect and quantify many proteins at single-cell level.

 

Here we present several commonly used applications, specifically in the study of cancer stem cells. The focus will be on the technical aspects of flow cytometric analyses including the preparation of biological specimens. We will also discuss the implications of various equipment parameters to facilitate experimental design. 

 

Biography

Dr Tang graduated from The Scripps Research Institute, USA, in 1998 with a Ph.D. in Macromolecular and Cellular Structure and Chemistry. Her postdoctoral training was carried out at The Scripps Research Institute (USA), Genomics Institute of Novartis Research Foundation (USA), and Howard Florey Institute (Melbourne). She joined National Neuroscience Institute in July 2005 to initiate work on brain tumor stem cell biology. The lab works in close conjunction with Dr Christopher Beng-Ti Ang, Associate Consultant, Department of Neurosurgery, NNI and Clinical Investigator, Singapore Institute for Clinical Sciences, Agency for Science, Technology & Research.

Research Scientist, Imaging Informatics Group, Bioinformatics Institute

Wed 30th Jan 1615-1700

 

Abstract

3D Time-lapse Imaging of developmental processes in the fruit fly Drosophila using the LSM 5 Live Microscopy System

 

Imaging the dynamics of live cells in multiple dimensions will significantly advance our understanding of biology. However the vast amounts of data produced by 3D time-lapse microscopy (typically in the double digit Gigabyte range) cannot be analyzed by human operators using traditional imaging software in an efficient and reproducible manner. To accelerate the interpretation of high-content microscopy data, my group aims to develop software solutions that apply computer vision methods to automate the image analysis pipeline. We produce the bulk of our 3D movies using the Zeiss LSM 5 Live high-speed laser scanning confocal microscope located at the BSF. With the help of fluorescently tagged fusion proteins we follow two major processes in Drosophila development. In embryos, we are interested in tracking cell migration and cell cycle progression. During metamorphosis, we study apoptosis and remodeling of the musculature. Following image acquisition, we perform a series of image processing and computer vision methods, such as deconvolution, segmentation, feature extraction, object tracking and classification. I will illustrate how multi-dimensional microscopy in combination with a quantitative analysis of cellular feature can help us gain novel insights into biological processes.

Abstract

Visualizing ultra-fast live cell dynamics  evolution of confocal laser scanning microscopy from point to line scanning

 

With the development of fluorescent indicators and recombinant proteins, the confocal laser scanning microscope (CLSM) has become widely established as a research instrument. First generation of CLSM is based on point-scanning mechanism which mainly used for multi-fluorescent fixed sample. Nevertheless, the problem of autofluorescence and excitation crosstalk (e.g. GFP + FITC) still could not be solved until 2002, whereas there is a breakthrough of PMT-array detector which can capture spectral imaging in fast simultaneous acquisition manner.

 

The steady investigation such as 2D or 3D imaging cannot fulfill scientist who requires monitoring of the temporal and spatial changes of biologically active molecules  live cell imaging. For visualizing dynamic process at 4D (xyzt), the ultra-fast confocal line scanner (capable of scanning up to 120 fps at 512x512 pixels) offers a temporal resolution which is impossible to achieve with point-scanning systems, therefore making it an ideal tool to study fast kinetics (Engelmann R, Nature Methods, Application Notes iii). To visualize the fast movements (Galbraith CG et al., Science, 315: 992), metabolic events such as fast calcium imaging (Song LS  et al., Ann NY Acad Sci, 1047:99) or developmental growth (Liebling M et al., J Biomed Optics, 10:054001-1), the line-scanning brings your research to life. Photo-bleaching experiments such as FRAP and FLIP provide you with a flexible tool for molecular kinetics studies (Tunnemann G, et al., FASEB J, 20:1775). With such superior scanning speed, application of live cell screening is also possible at multiple locations.

Dr. Samuel Ko studied Biochemistry in Hong Kong with a special focus on the cellular response of tumor necrosis factor-alpha-induced apoptosis in mouse fibroblast cell line L929 during his MPhil and PhD research, by using fluorescent imaging techniques such as Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy (CLSM). He received his PhD from The Chinese University of Hong Kong in 2001. Abstract

Three-dimensional microscopy by confocal imaging

 

Confocal microscopy is an established technique for imaging of biological specimens such as cells and tissue samples, and is also emerging as a method for non-invasive medical diagnosis. The important characteristic of confocal imaging is that an image is formed of a thin optical section through a thick sample. This property can be used to form three-dimensional images, and also has the result of rejecting scattered light when focusing deep into tissue (confocal gating). The basic principles of optical sectioning in different types of confocal microscope, including slit scanning, spinning disc and structured illumination instruments will be described and compared

Abstract

Analyzing nuclear genome organization and remodeling by using confocal microscopy

 

Mammalian cell nuclei display a characteristic spatial organization of chromatin domains and gene loci. This spatial organization is closely linked to their functional regulation. Thus, gene loci are only transcribed in a specific compartment in the nuclear interior and certain genes occupy different and characteristic nuclear positions depending on their transcriptional activity. Also, the replication timing of chromatin domains and gene loci is closely related to their nuclear positioning. In human nuclei we could directly visualize the dynamic interaction between the replication machinery and replicating DNA by applying high-resolution live cell microscopy and we could show that the spatial positioning of DNA in mammalian nuclei is stably maintained during such dynamic processes.

Abstract

High content Fluorescent-based screening platform in deciphering dengue virus biology

 

Dengue virus is a mosquito-borne flavivirus that represents an important emerging infectious disease and is an international health concern. Currently, there is no vaccine or effective antiviral drug to prevent or to treat dengue virus infection. The development of specific anti-dengue molecules would be facilitated by the availability of efficient anti-dengue screening assays, adaptable to high-throughput format. Abstract

Optimizing Instrumentation design in High Content Screening

 

High Content Screening is seeing significant interest in cellular imaging application that can involve automated processes in both the imaging and analysis of fluorescently labelled cells within academic research and drug discovery.  Significant barriers to high-throughput live cell imaging have now been overcome with the addition of precision environmental controls. Maintaining live cells at or near physiological conditions is a challenging but essential requirement for the success of many screening programs.

 

Potential cell stress is reduced even further by the use of Nipkow spinning disk imaging technology, which minimizes photo toxicity while enabling high speed confocal imaging.  High through put can also be advanced by the inclusion simultaneous signal detection and multiplexed computer based image processing, these new technologies make high-throughput live cell imaging both practical and reliable. This presentation will review aspects of HCS instrument design  in terms optimization for  live cell imaging.

 

Abstract

Advances in the tools for Multi-dimensional imaging

 

The ability of confocal systems and more recently many novel approaches to generating optical sections is opening up applications involving 3D and 4D image reconstruction. Although largely developed in parallel with confocal methods, the excessive photo-bleaching and slow scanning time through volume had severely limited the usefulness of  many these software based reconstruction methods. Now with the growth of high speed spinning disk confocal imaging along with a variety of other very low photo bleach fluorescence imaging methods the use of multi-dimensional reconstruction and 3D analysis methods is becoming an increasing important tool for both live and fixed cell investigations. This has been paralleled with the introduction of lower cost yet powerful computers as well as the rapid development in 3D game based graphic engines and cheap memory space; developments that now allow real time computer based reconstruction.

 

Imaging in 3 dimensions is a natural imaging state for humans, and the ability to apply this to cellular structures brings easy recognition and understanding to many biological processes. This coupled with advance image analysis techniques allows many of the powerful analysis tools for 2D images to be applied to 3D data sets, allowing analysis of cells in their true volume or 3D state.

Abstract

Isolation of Hematopoietic Stem Cells: From Hematological Reconstitution to Regenerative Medicine

 

Transplantation of hematopoietic stem cells (HSC) is the standard of care for various hematological malignancies. In recent years, mobilized peripheral blood stem cells (PBSC) have replaced marrow as the preferred source of HSC for allogeneic (typically for leukemia) or autologous (typically for lymphoma and myeloma) transplantation. During the last few years, with the availability of units from public cord blood (CB) banks, use of CB units is becoming popular (from pediatric to adult patients) because of the less stringent requirements in HLA matching. The problem of long engraftment times (for neutrophil and platelets) in CB transplantation is partially resolved by infusing multiple units into an individual patient. Enriched HSC has been used both in allogeneic (for the reduction of T lymphocytes that cause graft versus host disease) and autologous (for the purging of tumor cells in PBSC collection) transplantation.

 

HSC has been shown to contribute to repair other organs since 2002. Exciting new clinical applications in HSC for the improvement of cardiac function, liver regeneration and neural conditions (stroke and spinal cord injury) are constantly being developed. Careful and well-designed scientific research studies as well as strict adherence to national and international regulation requirements for the selection of HSC are both necessary so that the safety of patients and donors is ensured and maximal efficacy of such cell therapy is realized.

Abstract

From expression analysis to breast cancer biomarkers

 

The biological and clinical heterogeneity of breast cancer can be largely explained by information embedded within a complex but ordered transcriptional architecture. Comprising this architecture are gene expression networks reflecting biochemical and behavorial properties of tumors that might be harnessed to improve disease subtyping, patient prognosis and prediction of therapeutic response. The ability to parse out these gene networks, and assess their mechanistic and clinical implications, is a major interest in my laboratory. This presentation will discuss recent work in elucidating prognostic signatures in breast cancer that reflect the operational configuration of the TP53 pathway and delineate new clinical subtypes based on genetic grade. Novel analytical strategies that seek to pinpoint oncogenic mechanisms in breast cancer through integration of multiple forms of clinico-genomic information (expression, copy number, patient survival) will also be described.

Abstract

From Genes to Cells: Unraveling signaling pathways active in Mesenchymal Stem Cells (MSC)

 

To assess the properties of MSC we isolated and characterized three independent lines of marrow derived MSC and compared the properties of early passaged cells to late passage and differentiated adipocytes, ostocytes and chondrocytes derived from these MSC. Using Global gene expression profiling arrays to detect RNA transcripts coupled with bioinformatics tools, we have identified pathways that MSC use to differentiate into osteogenic, adipogenic and chondrogenic lineages. We have identified TGF-β signaling in a Smad-2/3 dependant manner, PDGF signaling & FGF signaling as the key pathways involved in MSC differentiation. The differentiation into these lineages is controlled by these cell signaling pathways & is affected when these pathways are perturbed by inhibitors of cell surface receptor function.

The approach of inhibiting signaling pathways in MSC enables us to assess their roles in MSC growth and differentiation and provides direct evidence of the importance of these pathways in MSC differentiation.

Abstract

Pathology of mice used in genetically engineering mouse experiment

 

Mouse pathology can be an important adjunct to medical research. Necropsy and histopathological techniques can be used for studying and characterizing the phenotype of genetically engineered mice (GEM), embryo anatomy and pathology, pathology of experimental manipulations, and pathology of aging mice. The background pathology of mice used in medical research can allow investigators to understand the normal phenotype of their mice and for comparisons with GEM. Commonly used lines of mice in GEM research include C57BL/6, 129, B6;129 and FVB. Many other strains and stocks are also used. Tumors are the main natural causes of illness and death of most lines of mice. These tumors include B-cell lymphomas, lung tumors, Harderian gland tumors and sarcomas. GEM can develop the same tumors in higher incidence earlier in life or often develop unique tumors in specific tissues, depending on the manipulated gene. Many GEM have non-neoplastic diseases involving any organ in the body. Conditions of the skin, kidney, intestine, liver, immune system, lung and brain are often described in GEM. GEM often have novel histopathologic lesions in specific tissues, not seen in normal mice. Mouse models of human diseases can be similar to those in humans but often are not exactly the same. The biology of mouse diseases is often similar to those in humans. This presentation will review the pathology of mice, and GEM in particular.

Abstract

Class Imaging: Classification of Breast Cancer Sections by MALDI Tissue Imaging

 

MALDI imaging is a novel technique providing unique molecular information to histological tissue sections. We applied MALDI imaging to a set of tissue sections from breast cancer patients to develop automatic tissue classification routines.

Abstract

Application of Immunohistochemistry for Mice

 

Gene expression in mouse tissues and cells can be studied in formalin fixed paraffin embedded tissues by the pathology technique of immunohistochemistry (IHC). Frozen sections and tissues fixed in other fixatives such as paraformaldehyde and Bouins can also be used. Thousands of antibodies to proteins and other antigens are available. IHC is most commonly used in human and veterinary pathology for tumor antigens, infectious agents and immunology. The optimal immunohistochemical protocol depends on fixative, antibody and antigen. Enhancement of immunostaining can be achieved with antigen retrieval using various methods including the use of enzyme digestion, a microwave, steamer, or pressure cooker. Retrieval solutions are also helpful. Optimal staining allows interpretation of antigen expression in specific cell organelles, cells and tissues. Antigen localization within cell organelles can be achieved with careful technique and interpretation. IHC can be used for characterization of normal protein expression in normal tissues of mouse embryos and adult mice, changes in expression with experimental protocols, and tumor cell characterization. Tumor diagnosis can be easy to achieve especially for lymphomas. IHC can serve as a valuable tool in medical research. The techniques are an artform and optimal results often can provide novel findings. This presentation will review and discuss all these areas of IHC.

Abstract

Identification of Phosphoproteins and Profiling of Phosphorylation Sites in Complex Biological Samples: A Simple and Efficient Workflow Using Mini-Gel-Separated Proteins

 

Although major advances have been achieved in the identification and quantification of protein phosphorylation sites by mass spectrometry (MS), routine, easily performed, and comprehensive quantitative analysis of protein phosphorylation still remains out of reach for many laboratories.  Here we report upon a simple fluorescence-based approach to quantify gel-separated phosphoproteins that is readily coupled with enrichment and identification of their sites of phosphorylation by MS.  An antibody-free process workflow is described involving orthogonal phosphomonoester-selective binding strategies.  First, sample complexity is reduced by selective enrichment of phosphorylated proteins from complex biological samples, such as A431-stimulated cell lysate, using affinity chromatographic medium of immobilized alkoxide-bridged dinuclear zinc complex at near neutral pH.  Then, the enriched proteins are separated by conventional gel electrophoresis and a phosphorylation-selective fluorescent stain is employed to selectively highlight phosphoproteins via binding to the phosphomonoester dianion moieties of serine, threonine, and tyrosine residues at neutral pH.   Interaction with other anionic residues, including carboxylate residues on proteins, is insignificant. As little as 1 ng of phosphoprotein is detectable by this method using standard charge-coupled device camera- or laser scanner-based imaging systems.  The detected phosphoprotein bands are excised, subjected to proteolytic digestion, and methylation of carboxyl groups. Constituent phosphopeptides are subsequently purified using titanium dioxide thin-film coated magnetic beads followed by direct analysis of phosphorylation sites by MALDI-TOF or tandem mass spectrometry.  Phosphopeptides can readily be identified from as little as 78 fmol of starting material.  The presented workflow significantly improved the coverage of identified phosphorylated proteins in complex biological samples.

Abstract

Phasing in the Home Laboratory

 

Many examples of S-SAD and Se-SAD phasing have been reported with diffraction data collected using copper radiation (1.54 Å) or radiation at the selenium K absorption edge (0.98 Å). With recent advances in X-ray technology, chromium radiation (2.29 Å) is now available for in-house data collection and appears to be ideally suited for measuring anomalous signals from weak anomalous scatterers such as sulfur, selenium, calcium and other atoms commonly found in protein crystals. The results of a number of successful SAD experiments using Cr radiation have been published by several groups including our own.

 

With the addition of Cr radiation to the crystallographers toolkit, in-house X-ray sources can provide at least two routinely useful wavelength options for macromolecular crystallography. This report also discusses the results of phasing by combining diffraction data collected using both Cu and Cr radiation sources. 

 

Finally, we report the results of data collection with a new imaging plate detector (R-AXIS HR) designed specifically for use with Cr radiation. This new detector allows the collection of data suitable for both phasing and refinement with Cr radiation from a single crystal in a single, simple diffraction experiment.
